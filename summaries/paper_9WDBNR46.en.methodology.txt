This study employs a systematic review methodology guided by the PRISMA framework to evaluate the integration of NLP models into corpus linguistics. The research design follows a structured screening process, starting with a broad search across Scopus and ACL Anthology for peer-reviewed literature published between 2013 and 2025. Keywords such as "AI in corpus linguistics" and "NLP corpus analysis" were used to identify relevant studies, with an initial pool of 922 records narrowed down to 12 after applying inclusion criteria focused on studies that apply NLP models like BERT and GPT to linguistic tasks.

The model and algorithm choices in the reviewed studies include transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT, GPT) and other NLP techniques such as keyword extraction and pragmatic annotation tools. These models were selected for their ability to handle large-scale corpus data, provide semantic depth, and automate annotation tasks. The study assumes that the selected models are representative of current NLP advancements in corpus linguistics, though it does not specify whether newer architectures like LLMs were included beyond the mentioned examples.

Baselines for comparison in the reviewed literature are not explicitly detailed in the abstract, which limits the assessment of model performance relative to traditional corpus linguistics methods. The study likely relies on qualitative comparisons of NLP-enhanced approaches against manual or rule-based corpus analysis techniques, though this is not explicitly stated. Future work could benefit from a more rigorous benchmarking framework to evaluate improvements in accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability.

The evaluation strategy involves a thematic synthesis of findings from the selected studies, supplemented by a quality assessment using the CASP checklist to ensure robustness. Thematic analysis is used to categorize applications of NLP models in corpus linguistics, such as discourse analysis, diachronic studies, and sociolinguistic variation. However, the abstract does not specify quantitative metrics or statistical analyses used to measure the impact of NLP models on corpus analysis outcomes. The study assumes that thematic synthesis is sufficient to capture the benefits and challenges, though a mixed-methods approach with quantitative validation could strengthen the findings.

Assumptions include the representativeness of the selected studies and the generalizability of NLP model applications across different linguistic tasks. The study also assumes that the PRISMA-guided approach ensures comprehensive coverage of relevant literature, though the abstract does not detail the specific inclusion/exclusion criteria beyond the application of NLP models. Future research could address these gaps by incorporating a broader range of models and providing clearer benchmarks for evaluation.
