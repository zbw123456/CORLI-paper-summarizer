This paper introduces a new dataset called Biasly, designed to detect subtle forms of misogyny in everyday language. Unlike previous datasets that focus on obvious insults, Biasly captures more nuanced and indirect expressions of sexism found in North American movie subtitles. The dataset was created with input from experts in multiple fields, including linguistics and social sciences, to ensure accuracy and depth in identifying these subtle biases.

The researchers collected and annotated movie subtitles to highlight misogynistic language that might otherwise go unnoticed. This dataset can be used for several natural language processing (NLP) tasks, such as classifying whether a text contains misogyny, measuring how severe the bias is, or even generating rewritten versions of biased text to remove the harmful language. The paper explains how the dataset was developed, analyzes the types of annotations collected, and provides baseline results using standard NLP models for detecting and mitigating misogyny.

The goal of this work is to contribute to AI that benefits society by improving the detection and removal of bias in language. By making this dataset available, the researchers hope to encourage further research in bias detection, explanation, and correction within NLP. The paper also emphasizes the importance of collaboration between different fields to ensure that the dataset reflects real-world language use and addresses the complexities of subtle sexism.
