This study looks at how well large language models (LLMs) handle text classification compared to smaller, more specialized models. The researchers tested three approaches using LLMs: zero-shot classification (where the model classifies text without any training examples), few-shot fine-tuning (where the model gets a small number of examples to learn from), and synthetic data generation (where the model creates its own training data to improve classification).

They tested these methods on 32 datasets covering 8 different languages. The results showed that zero-shot classification worked well for sentiment analysis (like determining if a review is positive or negative), but for other types of classification tasks, it didn’t perform as well as the other approaches. Using synthetic data from multiple LLMs often led to better classifiers than relying solely on zero-shot open LLMs. The study also found big differences in performance across languages, meaning some languages were easier for the models to classify than others.

Overall, the findings suggest that while LLMs are powerful, they aren’t always the best choice for text classification, especially for non-sentiment tasks or less common languages. Practitioners developing text classification systems should consider these trade-offs when choosing their approach.
