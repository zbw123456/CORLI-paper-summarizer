This paper explores how grammar might have developed in human language using reinforcement learning, a method where a system learns by trial and error to achieve goals. The authors focus on the idea that grammar evolves because speakers need to express different meanings in different situations. They model this process by assuming that, in any given context, there is a range of possible messages a speaker might want to convey, each with a certain likelihood.

The model breaks down language learning into small, manageable steps. At each step, the system uses the probabilities of different messages to guide its learning. This approach is tested through numerical simulations, which are like computer experiments that show how languages might evolve over time. The authors also provide mathematical proofs to support their findings. To show how this model could apply to real languages, they include two case studies from the history of English.

The paper suggests that grammar isn’t just a fixed set of rules but something that adapts based on what speakers need to communicate. By using reinforcement learning, the authors demonstrate how functional pressures—like the need to express different ideas—could shape the structure of language over time.
