The research design adopts a reinforcement learning framework to model the emergence of grammatical systems, focusing on how expressive purposes shape language evolution. The study assumes that speakers' communicative goals influence syntactic and semantic composition, integrating a probability distribution over possible messages in a given context. This distribution guides the learning and production algorithm, which decomposes language acquisition into sequential steps, each leveraging message probabilities to optimize communication.

The model employs a reinforcement learning approach where agents interact to learn grammatical structures. The algorithm iteratively updates production and comprehension policies based on feedback from communicative success, defined by the alignment between intended messages and interpreted utterances. The reinforcement signal is derived from the utility of expressing specific meanings in context, reflecting functionalist principles in linguistics.

For model choices, the study uses a Markov Decision Process (MDP) to formalize the learning environment, where states represent linguistic contexts, actions correspond to utterances, and rewards are tied to communicative accuracy. The policy is updated via a variant of Q-learning, adapted to handle the structured nature of language. The algorithm assumes bounded rationality, where agents make locally optimal decisions based on partial information.

Baselines include traditional symbolic grammar models and probabilistic grammar frameworks, such as Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and n-gram models. These baselines lack the dynamic, goal-driven adaptation central to the proposed model. The study also compares against simpler reinforcement learning agents that do not incorporate message probabilities, isolating the impact of functionalist pressures on grammatical emergence.

Evaluation relies on numerical simulations of language histories, tracking the evolution of grammatical rules over generations of agents. Analytic proofs complement simulations by establishing convergence properties and stability conditions for the learned grammars. Case studies from the history of English—such as the rise of periphrastic constructions—are used to validate the model's applicability to natural language change. The evaluation metrics include communicative success rates, grammatical complexity, and adaptability to new contexts.

Assumptions include the availability of a shared communication channel, a finite set of possible messages, and agents with bounded computational resources. The model does not address pragmatic factors like implicature or social conventions beyond the immediate communicative context. The case studies are illustrative rather than exhaustive, relying on historical data to demonstrate qualitative alignment with the model's predictions.
