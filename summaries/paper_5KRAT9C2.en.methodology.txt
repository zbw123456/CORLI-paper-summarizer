This paper investigates sources of hallucination in Large Language Models (LLMs) during Natural Language Inference (NLI) tasks through controlled behavioral experiments. The research design involves probing multiple LLM families (LLaMA, GPT-3.5, and PaLM) to identify systematic biases introduced during pretraining that contribute to hallucinations. The study focuses on two primary biases: sentence-level memorization and corpus-level statistical usage patterns.

The methodology employs controlled experiments to isolate the effects of these biases. For sentence-level memorization, the authors manipulate hypotheses to test whether models falsely label NLI samples as entailing when the hypothesis appears in training data, regardless of the premise. Entities are treated as indices to access memorized information, revealing a tendency to prioritize memorized content over contextual reasoning. For corpus-level statistical patterns, the study examines how the frequency of predicates in training data influences model predictions, particularly when the premise predicate is less frequent than the hypothesis predicate.

The evaluation strategy compares model performance on NLI test samples that conform to these biases versus those that do not. The hypothesis is that models will perform significantly worse on samples deviating from the identified biases, demonstrating their role as major sources of hallucination. The paper suggests these biases as critical controls for future LLM evaluations, emphasizing the need to account for pretraining artifacts in assessing model reliability.

Assumptions include the reliance on the availability of training data frequencies and hypothesis attestation in training corpora, which are not explicitly detailed in the abstract. The study does not specify the exact experimental setup for controlling variables or the metrics used to quantify hallucination rates, which would be necessary for reproducibility. Additionally, the paper does not mention comparisons to other potential sources of hallucination or baseline models beyond the selected LLM families.
