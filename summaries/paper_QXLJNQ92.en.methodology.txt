The research design focuses on evaluating whether large language models (LLMs) can distinguish between possible and impossible language by analyzing their internal probability distributions. The study employs a novel benchmark to elicit probabilities from four LLMs, computing minimal-pair surprisal differences across four conditions: lower frequency grammatical sentences, ungrammatical sentences, semantically odd sentences, and pragmatically odd sentences. The core prediction is that if string-probabilities reflect grammatical limits, ungrammatical sentences should exhibit uniquely high surprisal compared to other linguistic violations.

The model/algorithm choices involve selecting four LLMs to generate probability distributions for the benchmark sentences. The evaluation strategy relies on comparing surprisal differences between grammatical and ungrammatical conditions, as well as other types of linguistic oddities. The study assumes that surprisal, derived from model probabilities, can serve as a proxy for syntactic knowledge, though this assumption is explicitly tested.

Baselines include grammatical sentences as a reference point, with comparisons drawn against lower frequency grammatical sentences, ungrammatical sentences, semantically odd sentences, and pragmatically odd sentences. The evaluation measures the extent to which each condition deviates from the grammatical baseline in terms of surprisal.

The results indicate that ungrammatical sentences do not exhibit a unique surprisal signature compared to semantically or pragmatically odd sentences, suggesting that string-probabilities do not reliably capture model-internal representations of syntactic knowledge. This finding challenges prior claims about LLMs' ability to distinguish grammatical from ungrammatical language, advocating for alternative methodological approaches to assess syntactic competence in models.
