The research design of GPT-NER focuses on transforming the traditional sequence labeling task of Named Entity Recognition (NER) into a text generation problem to better leverage the capabilities of large language models (LLMs). The core methodology involves converting entity extraction into a generation task where entities are marked with special tokens (e.g., "@@Columbus##" for location entities). This approach aligns with the natural strengths of LLMs, which are inherently designed for text generation rather than sequence labeling. The study assumes that this transformation reduces the gap between NER and the generative nature of LLMs, enabling more effective adaptation.

For model and algorithm choices, GPT-NER utilizes LLMs as the primary architecture, specifically leveraging their text generation capabilities. The method employs a self-verification strategy to mitigate the "hallucination" issue, where LLMs may incorrectly label non-entities as entities. This is addressed by prompting the model to verify whether extracted entities belong to a predefined labeled tag, ensuring greater accuracy. The self-verification step is integrated into the generation process, acting as a post-processing mechanism to refine outputs.

The baselines for comparison include fully supervised NER models, which are considered the standard in traditional NER tasks. The study evaluates GPT-NER against these baselines across five widely adopted NER datasets to assess performance parity. Additionally, the research explores low-resource and few-shot scenarios, where the amount of training data is limited, to evaluate the model's adaptability and robustness in data-scarce environments.

The evaluation strategy involves comparing GPT-NER's performance with supervised baselines using standard NER metrics such as precision, recall, and F1-score. The study emphasizes the model's effectiveness in low-resource settings, where it demonstrates superior performance compared to supervised models. This suggests that GPT-NER is particularly well-suited for real-world applications where labeled data is scarce. The evaluation also includes qualitative analysis to understand the model's behavior in edge cases, such as handling ambiguous or noisy inputs.
