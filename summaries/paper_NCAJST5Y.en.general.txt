This study explores whether Whisper, a speech recognition tool, can help assess English proficiency in non-native speakers. The researchers used Whisper’s confidence scores—measures of how sure the tool is about its transcriptions—to classify learners by their skill level. They tested this on French, Italian, and German speakers reading English sentences.

The team found that Whisper’s confidence scores could group learners into levels, especially for beginners, using a simple algorithm called k-nearest neighbors. However, the tool’s language detection feature had limits. While it could sometimes guess a speaker’s native language (like French or German), this didn’t help much for advanced learners. The researchers also looked at how Whisper handles alternative transcriptions—different ways a word might be pronounced—and found that low confidence scores or unexpected transcriptions could hint at pronunciation mistakes.

The study used two datasets: one with French learners reading aloud, and another with Italian and German learners. By aligning Whisper’s output with the actual speech, they showed how confidence scores might be useful for giving feedback to learners or improving language learning tools. The findings suggest Whisper could be a practical, low-cost way to support language assessment, though it works best for less advanced speakers.
