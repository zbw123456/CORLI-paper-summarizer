The research design employs a diachronic corpus-based approach to analyze syntactic language change in English and German over approximately 160 years, focusing on parliamentary debates. The study utilizes five dependency parsers, including Stanford CoreNLP and four newer alternatives, to parse historical corpora while accounting for potential noise such as spelling variations and OCR errors. The choice of multiple parsers is motivated by the need to assess robustness and sensitivity of syntactic metrics to parser-specific biases, addressing a gap in prior work that often relies on single parsers.

The model/algorithm selection includes five dependency parsers to generate syntactic representations, enabling a comparative analysis of their outputs. The study evaluates 15 syntactic metrics related to dependency distance minimization (DDM) and tree graph properties, such as dependency distance, tree height, and degree variance. These metrics are selected to capture different aspects of syntactic complexity and processing efficiency, providing a comprehensive view of syntactic change.

Baselines for comparison are not explicitly defined in the abstract, but the study implicitly compares results across parsers and languages. The evaluation strategy involves tracking trends in syntactic metrics over time for both English and German, with a focus on identifying convergences or divergences in diachronic changes. The analysis also examines the relationship between syntactic changes and sentence length distributions, particularly at the tails, to explore whether changes are more pronounced in longer or shorter sentences.

Assumptions include the representativeness of parliamentary debates as a corpus for syntactic change and the adequacy of the selected parsers in handling historical data. The study assumes that the chosen metrics are valid indicators of syntactic complexity and processing difficulty, despite potential parser-induced variations. The robustness analysis across parsers and languages aims to mitigate these concerns, ensuring more reliable conclusions about syntactic language change.
