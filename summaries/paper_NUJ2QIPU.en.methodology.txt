The research design of ColorConceptBench centers on evaluating the probabilistic association between implicit color concepts and their visual representations in text-to-image (T2I) models. The benchmark introduces 1,281 implicit color concepts, each grounded in 6,369 human annotations, to probe how models interpret abstract color semantics beyond explicit color terms. The study employs a human-annotated dataset to capture nuanced color-concept relationships, ensuring that the evaluation reflects real-world variability in color perception.

For model evaluation, seven leading T2I models are tested, including both open-source and proprietary systems. The selection spans architectures such as diffusion-based models (e.g., Stable Diffusion variants) and autoregressive models (e.g., Parti), covering a range of training scales and conditioning mechanisms. The study assesses these models' ability to generate images that align with probabilistic color distributions derived from human annotations, rather than relying on exact color matches.

Baselines include standard T2I models without additional interventions, as well as models fine-tuned on color-specific datasets. The evaluation also incorporates common techniques like classifier-free guidance and prompt engineering to test their impact on color-concept alignment. However, the results indicate that these interventions fail to significantly improve performance, suggesting a fundamental limitation in how current models encode implicit color semantics.

The evaluation strategy employs both automated metrics and human judgment. Automated metrics include color distribution similarity measures (e.g., Earth Mover’s Distance) and concept alignment scores based on CLIP embeddings. Human evaluation is conducted via crowdsourcing, where annotators rate the consistency between generated images and the target color-concept pairs. This dual approach ensures both quantitative rigor and qualitative validation of the models' probabilistic color understanding.

Assumptions include the representativeness of the human annotations for capturing implicit color concepts and the generalizability of the benchmark to broader color semantics. The study does not assume that explicit color terms (e.g., "red") are sufficient proxies for implicit concepts (e.g., "passion"), as the benchmark explicitly avoids such simplifications. Instead, it focuses on the probabilistic nature of color associations, where multiple hues may correspond to a single concept.
