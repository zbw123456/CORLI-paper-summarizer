This study investigates the use of Whisper’s confidence scores for scoring L2 English learners and classifying their proficiency levels. The research design involves two primary datasets: the ANGLISH corpus for French learners and the ISLE corpus for Italian and German learners. The ANGLISH corpus is a graded read speech dataset, while the ISLE corpus includes spontaneous speech from learners at varying proficiency levels. The authors assume that Whisper’s language detection and subtoken probability scores can serve as proxies for learner proficiency, though this is not explicitly validated in the abstract.

For model choices, the study leverages the C++ re-implementation of Whisper, focusing on its language prediction and probability outputs. The k-nearest neighbors (kNN) algorithm is employed to classify learners into proficiency levels based on these confidence scores. The authors also explore the limitations of Whisper’s language detection probability, noting that it may inadvertently predict the learner’s native language (L1) rather than their proficiency level, particularly beyond a certain threshold.

The evaluation strategy includes testing Whisper’s multilingual models on the ISLE corpus to assess their ability to detect L1 and classify learner levels. A greedy alignment algorithm is used to analyze confidence scores assigned to Whisper’s subtokens, with the goal of identifying pronunciation errors. Low confidence scores and alternative transcriptions are proposed as indicators of learner difficulties, though the abstract does not specify how these cues are quantitatively validated.

Baselines for comparison are not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, which limits the assessment of Whisper’s performance relative to existing L2 scoring systems. The study’s findings suggest that Whisper’s confidence scores can be useful for initial proficiency classification but may not reliably distinguish advanced learners. The authors also highlight the potential for using Whisper’s outputs to provide learner feedback, though the practical implementation and effectiveness of such feedback are not detailed.
