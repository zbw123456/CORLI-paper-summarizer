Large language models like GPT are great at generating text, but they struggle with named entity recognition (NER), where the goal is to identify specific words in a sentence that belong to categories like people, places, or organizations. Traditional NER systems treat this as a labeling task, which doesn’t align well with how these models work. To bridge this gap, the researchers behind GPT-NER propose a clever workaround.

Instead of asking the model to label words directly, GPT-NER reformats the task as a text-generation problem. For example, if the goal is to find locations in the sentence "Columbus is a city," the model is prompted to generate a modified version of the sentence where the location is wrapped in special markers, like "@@Columbus## is a city." This way, the model can use its strengths in generating text to solve the problem indirectly.

One major challenge with this approach is that large language models sometimes "hallucinate," meaning they confidently identify entities even when none exist in the text. To fix this, the researchers introduce a self-verification step. After extracting potential entities, the model is asked to double-check whether each identified entity truly matches the category it’s supposed to belong to. This helps reduce false positives and improves accuracy.

The team tested GPT-NER on five standard NER datasets and found that it performs just as well as traditional supervised models, which are trained on large amounts of labeled data. What’s more impressive is that GPT-NER excels in low-resource and few-shot scenarios, where training data is scarce. In these cases, it significantly outperforms supervised models, making it a practical choice for real-world applications where labeled examples are hard to come by.
