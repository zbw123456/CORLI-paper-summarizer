Large Language Models (LLMs) like me are often praised for their ability to understand and generate human-like text. One key test of this ability is Natural Language Inference (NLI), where a model must determine if a given statement (the hypothesis) logically follows from another statement (the premise). For example, if the premise is "A dog is barking," a correct inference might be "An animal is making noise." NLI is important for tasks like answering questions or summarizing articles, where understanding relationships between sentences is crucial.

However, this new study shows that LLMs often struggle with NLI in ways that reveal hidden flaws. The researchers ran controlled experiments on several popular LLMs, including LLaMA, GPT-3.5, and PaLM. They found two major sources of errors—what they call "hallucinations"—where the models confidently produce incorrect answers.

First, the models sometimes rely too much on memorization. If a hypothesis (like "The Eiffel Tower is in Paris") appears frequently in the training data, the model may ignore the premise entirely and just say the hypothesis is true. It treats specific words or names as shortcuts to recall memorized facts, even when those facts don’t logically follow from the given context.

Second, the models are influenced by how often certain words or phrases appear together in general text. If a word in the hypothesis (like "run") is more common than a word in the premise (like "sprint"), the model may incorrectly assume the hypothesis follows from the premise, just because it’s used to seeing those words together more often.

The study found that LLMs perform much worse on NLI tasks that don’t match these biases—meaning they rely heavily on memorized phrases and common word patterns rather than true logical reasoning. The researchers suggest that future tests of LLMs should include more examples that challenge these biases, to better measure real understanding instead of shortcuts.

In short, while LLMs can mimic logical reasoning, they often fall back on memorization and statistical patterns from their training data. This makes them unreliable for tasks that require careful, context-sensitive inference.
