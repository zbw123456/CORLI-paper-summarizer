SiDiaC employs a structured research design to construct the first Sinhala diachronic corpus spanning the 5th to 20th centuries CE. The corpus was curated by filtering 46 literary works based on availability, authorship, copyright compliance, and data attribution, resulting in 58k words. Texts were sourced from the National Library of Sri Lanka and digitised using Google Document AI OCR, followed by post-processing to correct formatting and modernise orthography. Syntactic annotation and text normalisation strategies were adapted from FarPaHC, a related corpus for low-resource languages, to ensure consistency.

The corpus is annotated with two layers of genre categorisation. The primary layer uses a binary classification (Non-Fiction or Fiction), while the secondary layer includes more granular genres such as Religious, History, Poetry, Language, and Medical. Date annotations are based on written dates, though the authors acknowledge reliance on secondary sources for dating rare texts, which introduces potential uncertainty in temporal accuracy.

For evaluation, the corpus serves as a foundational resource for Sinhala NLP, enabling diachronic studies in lexical change, neologism tracking, historical syntax, and corpus-based lexicography. The authors do not specify a formal evaluation metric for the corpus itself, as its primary purpose is resource creation rather than model benchmarking. However, the corpus is designed to support downstream NLP tasks, implying that future work may evaluate its utility in tasks like historical language modelling or syntactic parsing.

Assumptions include the accuracy of OCR post-processing and the reliability of secondary date sources. The corpus does not include explicit inter-annotator agreement metrics for genre or date annotations, which may be a limitation for reproducibility. The methodology prioritises accessibility and standardisation over rigorous annotation validation, given the constraints of low-resource language processing.
