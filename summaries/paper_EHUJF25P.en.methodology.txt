The research design focuses on addressing the challenge of semantic prosody in machine translation, specifically for English-Chinese passive structures. The study assumes that semantic prosody, which refers to the consistent positive or negative connotations associated with a linguistic unit through collocation, is distinct from literal semantic meaning. The authors hypothesize that machine translation models often fail to capture these nuanced connotations, leading to translations that misrepresent the intended sentiment or tone.

To test this hypothesis, the authors create a specialized dataset of English-Chinese sentence pairs, explicitly designed to demonstrate the negative semantic prosody of Chinese BEI passives. The dataset likely includes sentences where the BEI passive construction is used in unfavourable contexts, as well as neutral or favourable contexts where its use would be inappropriate. The dataset construction process is not detailed in the abstract, so assumptions are made about the balance of examples, the diversity of contexts, and the annotation process for semantic prosody.

The model choices include three pre-trained machine translation models: OPUS-MT, NLLB-600M, and mBART50. These models are selected for their multilingual capabilities and varying architectures, which may influence their ability to learn semantic prosody. OPUS-MT is a collection of open-source models, NLLB-600M is a large multilingual model from Meta, and mBART50 is a multilingual sequence-to-sequence model. The authors fine-tune these models on the constructed dataset to adapt them to the specific task of translating English sentences into Chinese while accounting for semantic prosody.

The evaluation strategy involves comparing the performance of the fine-tuned models against their base versions. The primary metric is likely the accuracy of the models in using BEI passives appropriately based on the sentiment of the context.
