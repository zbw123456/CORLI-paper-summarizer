The research design employs a three-dimensional framework to systematically evaluate lexical semantic change across multiple corpora. The study integrates sentiment, breadth, and intensity as core dimensions, with additional consideration of word frequency shifts and thematic collocate changes. This design addresses the multidimensional nature of semantic change identified in historical linguistics while providing a unified computational approach.

For model and algorithm choices, the methodology relies on word embeddings to capture semantic shifts over time. The framework assumes that embeddings can represent the three primary dimensions—sentiment, breadth, and intensity—through vector space transformations. Sentiment is operationalized via sentiment analysis tools applied to word vectors, while breadth is measured through changes in the dispersion of word meanings across contexts. Intensity is assessed by examining shifts in the magnitude of word associations within the embedding space.

Baselines for comparison include static embedding models trained on individual time slices of the corpora, as well as dynamic embedding models that account for temporal evolution. The study also incorporates traditional corpus linguistics methods, such as collocation analysis, to validate computational findings. Frequency shifts are evaluated using standard corpus statistics to contextualize semantic changes.

The evaluation strategy combines quantitative and qualitative approaches. Quantitative metrics include cosine similarity between word vectors across time periods to measure semantic drift, as well as sentiment and intensity scores derived from embedding projections. Breadth is assessed through measures of semantic dispersion, such as the variance in word contexts. Qualitative validation involves thematic analysis of collocates to interpret shifts in word usage, particularly in relation to mental health and illness terminology.

The study applies this framework to two corpora: one focused on historical medical texts and another on contemporary discourse. The analysis demonstrates how semantic changes in mental health-related terms reflect broader social phenomena, such as pathologization and stigma. The methodology assumes that the corpora are sufficiently large and temporally diverse to capture meaningful shifts, though the abstract does not specify corpus sizes or time spans. The findings are contextualized within computational social science, emphasizing the framework’s applicability to studying societal trends through language evolution.
