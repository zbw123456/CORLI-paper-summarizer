The research design of this study centers on an iterative data curation framework designed to systematically improve data accuracy in large datasets where manual curation is impractical. The core methodology involves a continuous, feedback-driven process that integrates error detection and correction mechanisms. The framework is structured to operate in cycles, where each iteration applies a series of tests to identify inaccuracies, followed by targeted corrections. The theoretical foundation of the approach is built on probabilistic guarantees, ensuring that the error reduction process accelerates over time and that all errors are asymptotically eliminated with probability one. This is achieved through the use of statistical tests and adaptive sampling strategies that prioritize high-impact corrections.

The model/algorithm choices are guided by the need for scalability and robustness. The framework employs a combination of hypothesis testing and machine learning-based error detection to identify potential inaccuracies. Hypothesis tests are used to flag data points that deviate significantly from expected patterns, while machine learning models, trained on subsets of verified data, assist in predicting and correcting errors. The iterative nature of the process allows the models to refine their accuracy over time, leveraging feedback from each correction cycle. The choice of algorithms is driven by their ability to handle large-scale data efficiently, with a focus on methods that provide interpretable results to facilitate manual verification when necessary.

Baselines for comparison include traditional static data curation methods, such as one-time manual reviews or rule-based cleaning, as well as semi-automated approaches like crowdsourcing or active learning. The proposed framework is also compared against state-of-the-art automated data cleaning tools that rely on statistical outlier detection or consistency checks. The evaluation strategy is designed to assess both the efficiency and effectiveness of the iterative curation process. Efficiency is measured by the computational cost and scalability of the framework, while effectiveness is evaluated through metrics such as error reduction rate, precision and recall of error detection, and the proportion of errors corrected over time. Simulations are used to validate the theoretical guarantees under controlled conditions, while a real-world use case demonstrates the practical applicability of the framework. The real-world evaluation involves a dataset with known inaccuracies, allowing for a direct comparison between the proposed method and baseline approaches in terms of error elimination and computational feasibility.
