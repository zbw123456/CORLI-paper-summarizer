The study explores how the emotional meaning of words changes over time in English and German. To analyze this, researchers used a system called Valence-Arousal-Dominance (VAD), which measures three aspects of emotion: how positive or negative a word feels (valence), how intense the emotion is (arousal), and how much control the word conveys (dominance). Unlike simpler methods that only track whether a word is positive or negative, VAD provides a more detailed picture of emotion.

The researchers adapted two common algorithms to work with VAD, allowing them to estimate historical emotions in older texts. To test their methods, they created a new benchmark dataset with human-annotated emotions for historical words in both languages. This dataset was developed by experts familiar with the language from different time periods.

A key finding challenges earlier assumptions. Some previous studies suggested that carefully selecting a small set of "stable" seed words—words believed to have consistent emotional meaning over time—would improve results. However, this study shows that using such seed words actually reduces accuracy. Instead, the researchers found that using a larger, more diverse set of seed words leads to better performance in estimating historical emotions.

This work highlights the importance of considering how language evolves when analyzing historical texts. It also provides practical tools and data for future research in historical linguistics and computational analysis of emotions in older writings.
