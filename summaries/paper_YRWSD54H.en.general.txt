SiDiaC is a new digital collection of old Sinhala writing. It brings together 58,000 words from 46 different books written between the 5th and the 20th centuries. The goal is to give researchers and the public a single place to study how Sinhala language and style have changed over 1,500 years.

All the texts come from the National Library of Sri Lanka. First, the pages were scanned and turned into text using Google’s Document AI OCR tool. Then the team cleaned up the results and updated the spelling so modern readers can follow along. Because Sinhala is a low-resource language, the creators borrowed ideas from similar projects like FarPaHC to decide how to organise the words and sentences.

Each book is tagged twice. The first tag is simple: it says whether the book is Non-Fiction or Fiction. The second tag is more detailed and puts each work into one of five categories—Religious, History, Poetry, Language, or Medical. This two-layer system helps users find exactly the kind of text they need.

Building SiDiaC was not easy. Many old books are rare and hard to locate. Dates are sometimes uncertain because the original manuscripts do not always say when they were written. Still, the corpus is ready to use. It opens the door to new studies of word meanings, new word creation, sentence patterns, and dictionary-making for Sinhala.
