ColorConceptBench is a new tool designed to test how well text-to-image models understand colors when they’re not directly named. Most models today can handle straightforward requests like “a red apple,” but they struggle when colors are tied to abstract ideas, such as “a regal purple” or “a muted teal.” The benchmark fills this gap by focusing on 1,281 implicit color concepts, each backed by 6,369 human annotations to ensure accuracy.

The researchers tested seven leading text-to-image models using ColorConceptBench. Their findings show that even advanced models often miss the subtle meanings behind color descriptions. For example, a model might produce a bright purple when asked for a “sophisticated purple,” failing to capture the refined tone implied by the word “sophisticated.” Surprisingly, common fixes like increasing model size or adjusting guidance settings didn’t improve performance. This suggests that the issue isn’t just about scale—it’s about how these models learn and represent abstract concepts in the first place.

The paper highlights a key limitation in current text-to-image technology: while models excel at literal interpretations, they lack the nuanced understanding humans have when colors are linked to emotions, status, or cultural meanings. Addressing this gap will likely require rethinking how models are trained and how they process implicit information. For now, ColorConceptBench provides a clear way to measure progress in this area.
