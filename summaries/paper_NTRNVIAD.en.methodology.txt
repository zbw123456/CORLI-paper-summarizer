The research design focuses on analyzing the interaction between language usage dynamics and macroeconomic rhythms over the past two centuries in major Western languages. The study employs a mixed-methods approach, combining corpus linguistics, time series analysis, and mathematical modeling to uncover oscillatory patterns in word frequency data and their semantic coherence. The primary datasets consist of historical linguistic corpora from capitalist contexts, enabling long-term quantitative analysis. The research assumes that the observed cycles in word usage are not random but reflect underlying socio-economic processes, given the capitalist framing of the datasets.

The model choice centers on a minimal mathematical framework designed to capture word usage dynamics through parameters tied to real-world processes. The model incorporates a Hopf bifurcation to represent a critical regime where language usage exhibits heightened sensitivity to external factors. This choice is justified by the observed small-amplitude regular cycles in word frequency, which align with the behavior near a Hopf bifurcation. The parameters are fitted to empirical data to validate the model's predictive power and its ability to capture the interaction between language and economic activity.

Baselines for comparison include traditional time series models (e.g., ARIMA) and simpler harmonic oscillators to assess the explanatory power of the Hopf bifurcation model. Additional baselines may involve semantic clustering techniques (e.g., topic modeling) to evaluate the coherence of word usage cycles and their alignment with macroeconomic rhythms. The evaluation strategy prioritizes robustness checks, including cross-validation across different languages and time periods, sensitivity analysis of model parameters, and statistical tests for oscillatory coupling between language and economic data.

The study employs several evaluation metrics, such as the strength of oscillatory coupling (e.g., cross-correlation, Granger causality), the coherence of semantic clusters (e.g., topic coherence scores), and the goodness-of-fit of the mathematical model (e.g., R-squared, AIC/BIC). The primary hypothesis is that word usage operates near a Hopf bifurcation, and the coupling with economic activity is both robust and specific, manifesting as an oscillatory dynamic. The research also explores whether language usage precedes, tracks, or follows macroeconomic rhythms, providing insights into the feedback process between linguistic and material realities.
