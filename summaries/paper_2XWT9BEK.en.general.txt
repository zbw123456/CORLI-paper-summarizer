Idiomify is a tool designed to help non-native English learners understand and use idioms more effectively. An idiom is a phrase with a meaning that isn’t obvious from the words themselves, like "break a leg" (which means good luck, not to actually break a leg). Non-native learners often struggle with idioms because their meanings aren’t literal, and knowing when and how to use them can be tricky.

The project builds a reverse dictionary for idioms, which means users can describe a concept or situation in their own words, and the tool suggests idioms that fit. For example, if a learner wants to say something is very easy, they might type "very simple," and the tool could suggest the idiom "a piece of cake." To make this work well, the tool also includes collocations—common word pairs or phrases that help learners use idioms correctly. For instance, "piece of cake" often goes with "it’s a" or "piece of cake to do."

A major challenge is identifying idioms in real-world texts because they can appear in many forms. For example, "piece of cake" might be written as "piece of the cake" or "pieces of cake." The project solves this by creating rules based on the base form of idioms and then adjusting them to match variations in text.

To find collocations, the project tests three methods: Term Frequency (TF), which counts how often words appear together; Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI), which measures how much more often words appear together than by chance; and TF-IDF, a more advanced version that balances frequency with how unique the pair is. TF serves as a simple baseline, while PMI and TF-IDF are more sophisticated. The best results might come from combining these methods using machine learning to find the ideal balance.

For the reverse dictionary, the project uses Word2Vec, a tool that groups words and phrases based on their meanings in a way that captures their relationships. For example, it can understand that "piece of cake" is similar to "easy" or "simple." This approach is flexible and works well for suggesting idioms based on a user’s description.

The project also evaluates its methods and finds room for improvement. Adding techniques like slop (allowing small errors in matching), wildcards (using symbols to represent unknown words), and reordering (accounting for different word orders) could help identify idioms more accurately. Combining the reverse dictionary approach with an inverted index (a traditional search method) might also improve results.

While there are limits to the current methods, the project shows that they are practical and beneficial for non-native learners. The tool could be a valuable resource for anyone trying to master English idioms, making it easier to understand and use them in the right context.
