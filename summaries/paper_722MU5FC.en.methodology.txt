LARMOR is designed to address the challenge of unsupervised dense retriever selection under domain shift, where pre-trained dense retrievers may underperform on a target corpus due to differences in domain, task, or data distribution. The research design focuses on leveraging large language models (LLMs) to evaluate and rank dense retrievers without requiring labeled data from the target corpus.

The core methodology involves using LLMs to generate synthetic queries or pseudo-relevance feedback from the target corpus. These synthetic inputs are then used to evaluate the performance of multiple pre-trained dense retrievers in a zero-shot manner. The LLM assists in simulating relevance judgments or query-document interactions, enabling an unsupervised assessment of retriever effectiveness. The approach assumes that the LLM can provide meaningful signals about retriever performance despite the absence of ground-truth labels.

For model/algorithm choices, LARMOR employs a two-stage process. First, the LLM generates a set of synthetic queries or relevance signals based on the target corpus. Second, these synthetic inputs are used to score each dense retriever by measuring their retrieval quality, such as through pseudo-relevance metrics or query likelihood scores. The retriever with the highest synthetic performance is selected for downstream tasks. The method assumes that the LLM’s generated signals correlate with actual retriever performance on the target corpus, which may depend on the LLM’s generalizability and the quality of the synthetic data.

Baselines for comparison include traditional unsupervised retriever selection methods, such as using BM25 scores as a proxy for retriever performance or relying on metadata-based selection (e.g., retriever name or training domain). Other baselines may involve heuristic-based selection or random selection. The evaluation strategy focuses on measuring the retrieval effectiveness of the selected retriever on the target corpus, using standard IR metrics such as nDCG, MRR, or Recall. Since the target corpus is unlabeled, the evaluation relies on proxy metrics or synthetic relevance judgments generated by the LLM.

Assumptions include the availability of an LLM capable of generating meaningful synthetic queries or relevance signals, as well as the assumption that the synthetic performance metrics correlate with real-world retrieval effectiveness. The method also assumes that the target corpus is sufficiently representative of the domain or task to allow the LLM to generate useful signals. Limitations may arise if the LLM’s synthetic data is noisy or biased, leading to suboptimal retriever selection.
