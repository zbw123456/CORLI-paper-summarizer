The research design focuses on estimating historical word emotions in English and German using the Valence-Arousal-Dominance (VAD) scheme. The study adapts two popular word emotion induction algorithms to handle VAD annotations, addressing a gap in existing methods that primarily address polarity rather than the more nuanced VAD dimensions. The authors introduce a novel gold standard dataset for historical word emotions, manually annotated by scholars proficient in the respective language stages, covering both English and German. This dataset serves as the ground truth for evaluating the adapted algorithms in diachronic settings.

The model choices involve adapting two established word emotion induction methods to the VAD framework. The first adaptation likely builds on a distributional semantics approach, leveraging co-occurrence patterns in historical corpora to infer emotional connotations. The second adaptation may involve a graph-based method, such as label propagation, where seed words with known emotional values seed the propagation of emotional scores across a semantic network constructed from historical texts. The study explicitly tests the assumption that small, hand-selected seed word sets with supposedly stable emotional meanings improve model performance, a claim contradicted by the findings.

Baselines for comparison include traditional polarity-based sentiment analysis methods, which are adapted to the VAD scheme by mapping polarity scores to VAD dimensions or using existing VAD lexicons where available. Additionally, the study evaluates the performance of the adapted algorithms against a random baseline, where emotional scores are assigned without leveraging historical context or seed words. The gold standard dataset enables a robust evaluation of these models, with metrics such as mean squared error, correlation coefficients, and accuracy in predicting VAD dimensions across different historical periods.

The evaluation strategy employs cross-validation over time, ensuring that models trained on earlier language stages are tested on later stages and vice versa. This diachronic evaluation tests the generalization capability of the models across different historical periods. The study also conducts ablation experiments to isolate the impact of seed word selection strategies, comparing hand-selected seed sets against larger, randomly selected seed sets. The findings suggest that larger seed sets, which capture a broader range of emotional expressions, outperform hand-selected sets, challenging prior assumptions about the stability of emotional meanings in historical language.
