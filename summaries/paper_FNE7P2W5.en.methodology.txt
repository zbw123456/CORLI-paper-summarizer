The study employs a mixed-methods research design combining quantitative interaction logging with qualitative user feedback to evaluate how non-native English speakers (NNESs) interact with explainable AI paraphrasing tools. The primary research instrument is ParaScope, a paraphrasing assistant that integrates multiple information aids—back-translation, explanations, and usage examples—while recording user interactions. The system is designed to support iterative decision-making by providing both global and detailed information layers, allowing users to progress from high-level overviews to granular details as needed.

For model and algorithm choices, the paper does not explicitly describe the underlying paraphrasing model powering ParaScope. Assumptions include the use of a state-of-the-art generative language model fine-tuned for paraphrasing tasks, though the specific architecture, training data, or performance metrics are not disclosed. The information aids are implemented as modular components, with back-translation likely generated via a separate translation model, while explanations and usage examples are derived from model outputs or predefined templates. The study does not specify whether these aids are dynamically generated or precomputed.

Baselines for comparison are not explicitly defined in the abstract. Assumptions suggest that the study implicitly compares user interactions with ParaScope against a baseline condition where users rely solely on the raw AI-generated paraphrases without additional aids. However, the absence of a controlled baseline with a simpler interface or no aids limits the ability to isolate the effects of individual information aids. The paper also does not mention comparisons with existing commercial paraphrasing tools, which could provide context for the novelty of ParaScope’s design.

The evaluation strategy combines interaction logs with post-task surveys and interviews to capture both behavioral and attitudinal data. Key metrics include the frequency and duration of aid usage, the sequence in which aids are consulted, and the final acceptance rate of AI-generated paraphrases. User confidence, autonomy, and perceived writing efficiency are measured through Likert-scale questionnaires and qualitative feedback. The study also examines how language proficiency moderates the effectiveness of different aids, though the specific proficiency assessment method (e.g., TOEFL scores, self-reported fluency) is not detailed. Potential limitations include the small sample size (n=22) and the in-lab setting, which may not fully reflect real-world usage patterns. The paper does not address potential biases in user behavior due to the controlled environment or the Hawthorne effect.
