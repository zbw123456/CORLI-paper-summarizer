The research design of idiomify focuses on constructing a collocation-supplemented reverse dictionary of English idioms for non-native learners. The core challenge addressed is the automatic extraction of idioms from corpora, given their variability in forms. The approach involves deriving matching rules from base forms of idioms to identify their variations in text. This is achieved through pattern matching techniques, including the use of slop, wildcard, and reordering to account for idiomatic flexibility.

For collocation modeling, the study employs Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI) and Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) to quantify the significance of word pairs, as these metrics are widely recognized for such tasks. Term Frequency (TF) is used as a baseline for comparison. The rationale for selecting PMI and TF-IDF lies in their ability to capture both co-occurrence strength and contextual relevance, which are critical for identifying meaningful collocations in idiomatic expressions.

The reverse dictionary component is implemented using Word2Vec, a distributional semantics approach. This choice is justified by its flexibility and effectiveness as a baseline model. The study acknowledges that alternative approaches, such as inverted indices or graph-based methods, could also be viable but opts for Word2Vec due to its simplicity and robustness. The evaluation of the system reveals several areas for improvement, including the potential integration of inverted indices with distributional semantics to enhance performance.

Evaluation metrics are not explicitly detailed in the abstract, but the study suggests that improvements can be made by leveraging machine learning to optimize the balance between PMI and TF-IDF. The authors imply that a hybrid approach combining these metrics could yield better collocation extraction results. Additionally, the evaluation highlights the need for further refinement in idiom identification techniques, particularly in handling idiomatic variations more effectively.

The study assumes that the base forms of idioms are known or can be pre-defined, which is a critical assumption for deriving matching rules. It also assumes that the corpora used for mining idioms are sufficiently large and representative to capture idiomatic variations. The benefits of the proposed methods for non-native learners are evident, as the reverse dictionary and collocation guidance aim to facilitate idiom acquisition and usage. The feasibility of the methods is supported by their implementation and evaluation, though the authors acknowledge room for further enhancement.
