The research design employs a supervised learning approach to automate grammatical annotation using large language models (LLMs). The study focuses on the English evaluative verb construction 'consider X (as) (to be) Y', leveraging corpus data from Davies' NOW and EnTenTen21 (SketchEngine) to train and evaluate the model. The pipeline integrates prompt engineering, fine-tuning, and evaluation stages to ensure replicability and scalability.

The primary model choice is Claude 3.5 Sonnet, selected for its demonstrated performance in natural language processing tasks. The authors assume that the model's pre-trained knowledge of English grammar and syntax is sufficient for the annotation task, though no explicit comparison with other LLMs is provided. The method assumes that the construction's grammatical features can be reliably captured through supervised learning with minimal training data.

Baselines for comparison are not explicitly mentioned, though the paper implies that traditional manual annotation or rule-based systems could serve as alternatives. The evaluation strategy relies on a held-out test set to measure model accuracy, with the reported result exceeding 90%. The authors do not detail the size or composition of the training and test sets, nor do they discuss potential biases in the corpus data.

The generalisability of the method is discussed in the context of other grammatical constructions and variation studies, suggesting that the pipeline could be adapted with appropriate modifications. However, the paper acknowledges caveats, likely related to model interpretability, domain specificity, and the need for human oversight in validating annotations. The authors emphasize the role of AI as a "copilot" rather than a replacement for linguistic expertise.
