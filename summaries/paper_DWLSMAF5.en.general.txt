The study looks at how English grammar has changed over time, focusing on a specific structure called the "perfect." In modern English, the perfect tense is formed with "have" plus a past participle, like "I have eaten." But in earlier English, people also used "be" plus a past participle, like "I am gone." Over time, "have" became the dominant choice, except in a few cases.

The researchers wanted to understand why this change happened. Was it because of natural selection—where one form proved more useful and spread—or was it just random chance, like a word becoming popular without a clear reason? To find out, they analyzed a huge amount of text from three sources: EEBO (Early English Books Online), COHA (Corpus of Historical American English), and Google Books. These sources cover English from the 1400s to the present day.

They focused on intransitive verbs, which don’t take a direct object, like "go" or "arrive." Most of these verbs showed a clear shift from using "be" to using "have" over time. A computer model then classified these changes, and most were labeled as "selection," meaning the shift likely happened because the "have" form was more useful or efficient, not by accident.

This suggests that the evolution of English grammar wasn’t random but driven by natural selection, similar to how biological traits evolve. The findings also offer a new way to study how grammar changes in human languages over long periods.
