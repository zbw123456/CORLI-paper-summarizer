The research design of this study focuses on evaluating the few-shot learning capabilities of a large-scale autoregressive language model, GPT-3, without any gradient updates or fine-tuning. The primary goal is to assess whether scaling up language models can enable them to perform new tasks with only a few examples or instructions, mimicking human-like adaptability. The study tests GPT-3 on a diverse set of NLP tasks, including translation, question-answering, cloze tasks, and tasks requiring on-the-fly reasoning or domain adaptation, such as word unscrambling, novel word usage, and arithmetic operations. The model is evaluated in a zero-shot, one-shot, or few-shot setting, where task descriptions and demonstrations are provided purely through text interaction.

The model choice is GPT-3, an autoregressive language model with 175 billion parameters, which is 10 times larger than any previous non-sparse language model. The architecture follows the standard transformer-based design, leveraging self-attention mechanisms to process and generate text. The training process involves unsupervised pre-training on a large corpus of text, followed by no fine-tuning for the few-shot evaluation. The study assumes that the model’s performance improves with scale, as larger models can capture more complex patterns and generalize better from minimal examples.

For baselines, the study compares GPT-3’s few-shot performance against prior state-of-the-art fine-tuned models on various NLP benchmarks. While the abstract does not specify exact baselines, it implies that GPT-3 achieves competitive results without task-specific fine-tuning. The evaluation strategy involves testing the model on a wide range of datasets, including those from established NLP benchmarks like SuperGLUE, LAMBADA, and TriviaQA, as well as custom tasks designed to test reasoning and adaptation abilities. Human evaluations are also conducted to assess the quality of generated news articles, comparing them to human-written articles to measure indistinguishability.

The study identifies limitations, including datasets where GPT-3 struggles in the few-shot setting and methodological issues related to training on large web corpora, such as potential biases or inaccuracies in the training data. The broader societal impacts of large-scale language models, including ethical concerns and potential misuse, are also discussed. The research design emphasizes the potential of scaling language models for few-shot learning while acknowledging areas for improvement and further investigation.
