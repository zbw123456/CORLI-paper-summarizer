Semantic prosody is a subtle but important aspect of language that affects how words are understood based on the words they often appear with. For example, the word "cause" usually has a negative prosody because it often appears in sentences about problems or bad outcomes, like "the accident caused injuries." Even if a word's literal meaning is neutral, its frequent company can give it a positive or negative tone.

This study focuses on a specific structure in Chinese called the BEI passive, which is used to describe actions where the subject is affected negatively. For instance, "The book was read by him" in English might sound neutral, but in Chinese, using the BEI structure often carries a negative or unfavorable connotation. The researchers noticed that machine translation systems often miss these subtle differences, leading to translations that sound unnatural or incorrect in context.

To address this, the team created a dataset of English-Chinese sentence pairs where the BEI passive is used appropriately for unfavorable content. They then fine-tuned three machine translation models—OPUS-MT, NLLB-600M, and mBART50—using this dataset. Fine-tuning means they adjusted the models to better understand and apply the concept of semantic prosody in their translations.

The results showed that the fine-tuned models performed better at using the BEI passive structure when translating unfavorable content and avoided using it for neutral or favorable content. This improvement was especially clear in the NLLB-600M model, which is designed to handle multiple languages. Interestingly, the knowledge of semantic prosody learned from English-Chinese translation could even be transferred to other language pairs, such as Spanish-Chinese, showing that the model generalized the concept beyond just one language pair.

This research highlights the importance of teaching machine translation systems not just the words and grammar of a language, but also the subtle meanings that come from how words are used together. By doing so, translations can become more natural and accurate in real-world contexts.
