The idea that creativity requires human intention has long been a common belief. Many people think that for something to be truly creative, a person must have intended to create it. However, this paper argues that this requirement, which they call the Intentional Agency Condition (IAC), is no longer useful as a general rule for creativity. Instead, they suggest that creativity should be judged more by the results it produces rather than who or what created it.

The authors point out that advances in artificial intelligence have made this issue more pressing. AI systems can now generate art, music, and writing that many people find creative, even though these systems don’t have intentions or conscious goals. This challenges the traditional view that creativity must involve human intention. The paper provides evidence that people, including authors and journalists, are increasingly calling AI-generated works creative, which weakens the argument that intention is always necessary.

The authors also argue that the IAC no longer serves its original purpose. Instead of helping us identify and reward truly creative work, it might lead us to unfairly dismiss AI-generated creativity or overlook valuable outputs simply because they lack human intention. They propose replacing the IAC with a simpler rule: creativity should be judged by whether something is novel and valuable, regardless of who or what created it.

While they suggest abandoning the IAC as a general rule, they acknowledge that intention might still matter in certain specific contexts. For example, in legal or ethical discussions about ownership or credit, human intention could still play a role. But for the broader question of what counts as creative, the focus should shift to the quality and impact of the work itself.
