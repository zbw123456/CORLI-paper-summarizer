The research design for this paper employs a mixed-methods approach combining corpus analysis and conceptual engineering to evaluate the Intentional Agency Condition (IAC) in the context of generative AI. The study first examines linguistic trends in attributions of creativity to AI systems by analyzing a corpus of texts from authors and journalists. This corpus evidence is used to assess whether the IAC remains descriptively valid, given the observed shifts in how creativity is ascribed to AI despite its lack of intentional agency. The second component involves a conceptual analysis using the method of conceptual engineering, which evaluates the functional role of the IAC in social contexts. The goal is to determine whether the IAC still serves its intended purpose of identifying and encouraging reliable sources of novel and valuable products or if it now contributes to biases in assessing AI-generated outputs.

The model or framework guiding this research is conceptual engineering, which involves critically examining and revising conceptual frameworks to better align with their intended functions. In this case, the IAC is treated as a conceptual tool whose utility is assessed based on its ability to fulfill its core social function. The study assumes that the IAC has historically been used to distinguish between human and machine creativity, but recent advancements in AI challenge this distinction. The authors implicitly assume that the shift in linguistic intuitions and the biases introduced by the IAC are significant enough to warrant a reevaluation of its general applicability.

For baselines, the paper contrasts the IAC with the proposed consistency requirement, which ties creativity to the reliable generation of novel and valuable products rather than to intentional agency. The consistency requirement is introduced as a more functional alternative, and its implications are explored in comparison to the IAC. The evaluation strategy involves assessing the descriptive accuracy of the IAC (via corpus evidence) and its functional utility (via conceptual analysis). The study also considers specific domains where the IAC might still be relevant, such as contexts where intentional agency plays a crucial role in the creative process.

The evaluation of the IAC’s descriptive validity relies on the corpus analysis, which examines how often and in what contexts creativity is attributed to AI systems. The functional evaluation involves analyzing whether the IAC still effectively guides social practices related to creativity, such as recognition, encouragement, or critique of creative outputs. The paper does not provide explicit quantitative metrics for these evaluations but instead relies on qualitative assessments of linguistic trends and conceptual functions. The authors assume that the observed shifts in linguistic intuitions and the biases introduced by the IAC are sufficient to justify its abandonment at a general level, while acknowledging its potential relevance in specific local domains.
