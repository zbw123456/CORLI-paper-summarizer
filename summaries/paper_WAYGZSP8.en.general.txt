This paper introduces a practical method to help linguists annotate large text collections more efficiently. Traditionally, researchers manually label grammatical features in corpora, but as datasets grow, this becomes time-consuming and difficult to scale. The authors propose using a large language model (LLM) as an assistant to automate part of this process.

The method involves training the LLM to recognize specific grammatical patterns in English sentences. For their case study, they focused on the verb "consider" used in constructions like "consider X (as) (to be) Y," where X and Y are noun phrases or adjectives. They used two large English corpora—Davies' NOW and EnTenTen21—to train and test their model. By carefully designing prompts and fine-tuning the model with a small set of labeled examples, they achieved over 90% accuracy on unseen test data. This means the model can reliably identify and label the construction in new texts.

The authors suggest this approach could be adapted for other grammatical constructions or studies of language change. While the results are promising, they note some limitations. The method requires initial human effort to label training data and design prompts. Also, the model’s performance depends on the quality and representativeness of the training examples. Despite these caveats, they argue that LLMs can act as useful "copilots" for linguists, speeding up annotation tasks without replacing human expertise.

Overall, the paper offers a concrete and replicable workflow for using LLMs in corpus linguistics, making large-scale grammatical annotation more feasible.
