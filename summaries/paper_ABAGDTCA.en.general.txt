Translationese refers to the subtle linguistic differences that often appear in translated texts compared to original writing. For example, translated sentences might follow the grammatical structure of the source language more closely or use vocabulary in ways that feel unnatural in the target language. Traditionally, researchers have treated translationese as a simple yes-or-no question: is a text translated or original? But this paper argues that translationese exists on a spectrum—some texts are more "translated" than others—and introduces a new way to measure it.

The authors propose the translationese-index (T-index), a score that quantifies how much a text resembles translated language. Instead of a binary label, T-index gives a graded result, like a temperature reading, where higher values indicate stronger translationese traits. To calculate this score, they use two specialized language models (LMs). One model is fine-tuned on original texts, while the other is fine-tuned on translated texts. By comparing how likely each model finds a given text, the T-index reveals how "translated" the text sounds.

The researchers tested the T-index in two ways. First, they created synthetic translations and real-world translated texts to see if the score generalizes across different types of writing, authors, and language pairs. Second, they compared the T-index scores with human judgments—asking people to rate how translated a text felt—to ensure the scores align with human perception. The results showed that T-index works well even in new, unseen contexts, and it matches human assessments closely.

Interestingly, the T-index didn’t strongly correlate with existing machine translation quality metrics like BLEU or COMET. This suggests it measures something different—perhaps the "feel" of translationese rather than translation accuracy. The authors propose that T-index could complement these existing metrics, offering a new perspective in evaluating machine translation quality.

The study used relatively small language models (0.5 billion parameters) fine-tuned on just 1,000 to 5,000 text pairs, which makes the approach practical and efficient. Overall, this work shifts the focus from detecting translationese as a binary problem to measuring it on a scale, providing a more nuanced tool for linguists and translation researchers.
